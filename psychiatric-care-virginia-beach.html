What this is

Medication management, done with care

Psychiatric medication management is more than writing a prescription. It is a thorough evaluation of what is going on, a careful decision about whether medication is the right tool, and then ongoing follow-up to see how you are responding and adjust as needed. A lot of care moves fast: a few questions, a diagnosis, a prescription, and a check-in months later. That is not how we work. At Anchor Neuro, the focus is on getting the picture right and then walking alongside you, not handing you a bottle and a calendar reminder.

You might be carrying anxiety you cannot put down, a low mood that has lingered too long, a mind that will not slow down, or the weight of something that happened years ago. Whatever brought you here, you deserve a plan that fits your life and your goals. Medication can be a powerful part of that plan when it fits, and it is never the only tool we have.

Her approach

Evaluation, collaborative prescribing, and steady follow-up

Good medication management follows a rhythm. It starts with a thorough psychiatric evaluation: your history, your symptoms, your sleep, your stress, your goals, and what has and has not worked before. Only then does the question of medication even come up, and it comes up as a conversation, not a verdict.

When medication does make sense, Courtney prescribes thoughtfully. The guiding principle is “start low, go slow”: begin with a measured dose, give it time, and adjust based on how you actually respond rather than guessing. You have a say at every step. We explain why a medication is on the table, what to expect, the trade-offs, and how we will know whether it is helping. This is collaborative and measured care, not a prescription mill, and the plan is yours as much as ours.

Then we stay with you. Follow-up is where medication management is really earned: tracking how you feel, watching for side effects, and fine-tuning over time. When something is not working, we change it. When something is working, we protect it.

Honest about controlled medications

Controlled substances: careful, case by case

Some conditions, like ADHD, are sometimes treated with controlled medications such as stimulants. We do not advertise or promise any specific controlled medication. Prescribing decisions, including for controlled medications, are made individually after a clinical evaluation and follow Virginia and federal rules. Some may require an in-person visit or added monitoring, and we may request prior records as part of safe, responsible care. We do not provide early refills. The goal is care that is both effective and safe, for you and over the long run.

You can read the full medication and controlled-substance policy on our How it works page.

Medication and therapy together

Medication works best alongside therapy

Medication can lift the worst of the weight so the rest of the work becomes possible, but on its own it rarely does the whole job. The honest version is this: medication tends to work best alongside therapy, not instead of it. Courtney manages the medication side herself and coordinates your therapy: she refers you to a trusted, experienced therapist and stays your consistent point of contact, or coordinates with a therapist you already see. Trauma-focused approaches like Brainspotting are available through trusted referral partners and coordinated as part of your plan, and neurofeedback is offered in office as a complementary option when it fits your goals. Medication is one option within a plan, and we will always be straight with you about what it can and cannot do. There are no magic-pill promises here, and no guarantees that the right medication will simply fix everything.

A quiet difference

We measure whether medication is actually working

It is easy to lose track of progress when life is hard. So, when it is helpful, we use brief, validated check-ins like the PHQ-9 for depression or the GAD-7 for anxiety to see whether things are genuinely moving in the right direction over time. These take a minute or two, they are optional, and they are never a test you can pass or fail. They simply give us a clearer, honest picture so we can adjust your medication and your plan with real information instead of guesswork.

Questions, answered honestly

Medication management FAQ

Do you prescribe controlled substances or stimulants?

Prescribing decisions, including for controlled medications such as stimulants, are made individually after a clinical evaluation and follow Virginia and federal rules. Some may require an in-person visit or added monitoring, and we may request prior records as part of safe, responsible care. We do not advertise or promise any specific controlled medication, and we do not provide early refills. You can read the full medication and controlled-substance policy on our How it works page.

Is medication required to be a patient here?

No. Medication is one option within a plan, never a requirement. Some people benefit from it, some do well with therapy, neurofeedback, or Brainspotting, and many do best with a combination. We talk through the options together and you have a say in every decision. If medication does make sense, we explain why, what to expect, and how we will track whether it is helping.

Do you offer therapy too, or only medication?

Medication tends to work best alongside therapy, not instead of it. Courtney manages the medication side herself and coordinates your therapy: she refers you to a trusted, experienced therapist and stays your consistent point of contact, or coordinates with a therapist you already see. The goal is a complete plan, not a prescription in isolation.

Can medication management be done by telehealth?

Yes. Evaluations and medication follow-ups work well by secure telehealth anywhere in Virginia, which is convenient for busy schedules and for military families managing deployments and moves. Some prescriptions, particularly certain controlled medications, may require an in-person visit under Virginia and federal rules. You can also see us in person at our Virginia Beach office on Laskin Road.
