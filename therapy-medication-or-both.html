Getting care

Therapy, medication, or both? Making sense of your mental health options.

By Courtney Davis, DNP, APRN, PMHNP-BC, BCN · Updated June 2026

If you are weighing whether to try talk-based support, medication, or something brain-based, here is a calm, honest look at the main options and how the right plan is built around you.

Draft, pending final review before publishing.

When you finally decide to do something about how you have been feeling, the next question can be just as hard: what kind of help? Talk it through? Try a medication? Something newer you have heard about? It is easy to feel like you are supposed to already know the answer. You are not. Sorting through the options is part of getting care, and it is something we do together.

The honest truth is that there is no one-size-fits-all answer. Two people with the same diagnosis can need very different plans. So instead of selling you on a single approach, let's walk through the main ones, what each is good at, and how we decide.

The main paths, in plain terms

Talk and therapeutic approaches

This is the part most people picture: working through what you are carrying, building skills to handle stress, anxiety, or low moods, and understanding patterns so they have less of a grip on you. It can stand on its own, and it often makes everything else work better. At Anchor Neuro, Courtney coordinates your therapy and refers you to a trusted, experienced therapist when a dedicated course of therapy is the right next step, so you are not left to find one alone. She manages the medication side herself and stays your consistent point of contact, keeping the pieces of your plan connected.

Psychiatric medication

Medication can be genuinely helpful for the right person at the right time. For some people it turns down the volume on anxiety or lifts a heavy mood enough that the rest of the work becomes possible. It is not magic, and it is not the answer for everyone. We treat it as one tool, never a default, and never a requirement for being seen here.

When medication does make sense, we only consider it after a full evaluation, and we explain why, what to expect, and the trade-offs. We do not promise a specific drug or a specific result, and we adjust based on how you actually respond rather than guessing. The approach is collaborative and measured, often start low and go slow, and you have a say at every step. If another approach fits you better, we will tell you that honestly.

Prescribing decisions, including for controlled medications, are made individually after a clinical evaluation and follow Virginia and federal rules. Some situations may require an in-person visit or added monitoring, and we cannot promise any specific controlled medication in advance.

Brain-based approaches

These are the options people are often most curious about, and they deserve a measured explanation.

Neurofeedback is non-invasive training that helps your brain practice calmer, more regulated patterns. Sensors on the scalp only read your brain's activity. Nothing is sent into your brain. Research supports it as a tool that may help with focus, sleep, and regulation, and for trauma and PTSD the evidence is still emerging and promising rather than proven. It is offered in our Virginia Beach office only, since it uses equipment on site, and it works best as part of a broader plan.

Brainspotting is a newer brain-body approach, developed by David Grand, used to help process trauma and stress. Its research base is still developing. It is a trauma therapy we can connect you with through trusted referral partners and coordinate as part of your plan, not a guaranteed fix. It is delivered by a trained Brainspotting therapist, in person or by telehealth depending on that therapist's setting, while Courtney manages your medication care and keeps the plan coordinated.

So how do we choose?

We start with a whole-person evaluation. That means looking at more than a symptom checklist: your history, your sleep, your stress, your relationships, what has and has not helped before, and what you actually want to feel different. Then we build a plan around that. Sometimes the plan is one approach. Often it is a thoughtful blend, because these tools are not rivals. Medication, which Courtney manages herself, can steady things enough for the deeper work to land. Neurofeedback in our office, or trauma therapy like Brainspotting that we coordinate and connect you with through a trusted therapist, can support what medication alone cannot reach. The mix is yours, and it can change as you do.

An honest way to tell if it is working

A plan is only good if it helps, and feelings are hard to track from memory when life is busy. So when it is useful, we use brief, validated check-ins like the PHQ-9 for depression or the GAD-7 for anxiety. They take a minute or two, they are optional, and they are not a test you can pass or fail. This is what measurement-based care means: instead of guessing whether things are moving, we get a clearer picture over time and adjust with real information. If something is not helping, we would rather know early and change course.

How Anchor Neuro builds a plan with you

Your first step is a free consult, a short and no-pressure conversation to see if we are the right fit before any clinical visit. From there, the evaluation comes first, the options are explained in plain language, and the decisions are made with you, not handed to you. You can see us in person at our Virginia Beach office on Laskin Road or by secure telehealth anywhere in Virginia, which helps when work, family, or a military schedule makes getting to an office hard. Neurofeedback is the one service that is in-office only.

Questions, answered honestly

Do I have to take medication to get help?

No. Medication is one tool, not a requirement. Courtney provides psychiatric medication management herself, and she coordinates your therapy and refers you to a trusted, experienced therapist when talk-based or trauma-focused work is the right fit. Some people do well with therapy or with brain-based options like neurofeedback or Brainspotting, some benefit from medication, and many do best with a combination. We talk through the options together and you stay in the driver's seat. If medication does make sense, we explain why, what to expect, and how we will track whether it is helping.

Is it better to start with therapy or medication?

There is no single right order, because it depends on you. What you are dealing with, how severe it is, what has helped before, and what you are comfortable with all shape where we start. That is the point of a thorough evaluation: we build a plan around your situation rather than a default. Courtney manages the medication side herself and coordinates and refers you to trusted therapists for the therapy side, so the pieces work together. For some people that means starting with one approach, for others a blend from the beginning.

How will I know if my plan is actually working?

We watch how you feel in daily life, and when it is helpful we use brief, validated check-ins like the PHQ-9 for depression or the GAD-7 for anxiety to track changes over time. They take a minute or two, they are optional, and they are not a test you can pass or fail. They give us an honest picture so we can adjust your plan with real information instead of guesswork.
