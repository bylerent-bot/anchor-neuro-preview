⚓ Anchor Neuro Website & Growth Plan

A simple plan to grow Anchor Neuro.

A fresh website concept plus a practical, low-overwhelm roadmap for turning Anchor Neuro from a solo practice into a thriving group practice in Virginia Beach.

Put together by Patrick · a first draft to react to, nothing set in stone.

Where things stand, and the opportunity

Anchor Neuro has the hard part already in place: real clinical depth, an insurance path through Headway, and a calming brand. What's missing is visibility. Right now a new client basically has to already know your name to find you. The whole plan below is about fixing that, gently, without turning you into a marketer.

The site now leads with the two services that carry the practice: psychiatric medication management and trauma care, with the PTSD and veteran community as the human core. Neurofeedback and Brainspotting are still there, framed as complementary options that round out the care, not the headline.

Three things make this very winnable in Virginia Beach:

  • There's a real shortage of psychiatric prescribers. Demand for medication management is high and wait times are long, which works in your favor as you add capacity.
  • Hampton Roads is a trauma and veteran market like few others. A large active-duty and veteran population, and very few prescribers who lead with discreet, trauma-focused care.
  • Neurofeedback is rare locally. Few practices offer it, so it's a genuine point of difference, offered alongside medication and therapy rather than as the whole pitch.

1. The website: your home base

The attached concept keeps the calm, airy look you liked, and adds the things a growing practice needs that most provider sites skip: clear services led by medication management and trauma care, a real explainer for each, conditions you treat, insurance and booking, and a section for bringing on other clinicians.

Just as important: it should be easy for you to run. The site should live on a simple platform you (or an office admin) can edit in minutes, like Squarespace or your current Wix, no code required. Think of the polished design here as the look to recreate there.

A website only works if it tells the truth specifically. The few things that would make it really sing are yours to provide:

  • A real bio. The single most powerful thing on a practice site is a warm, specific story: your training, why you do this, your approach. People choose a provider they feel they already know.
  • Photos. A friendly headshot and a few calm, real images (your space, nature) instead of the placeholders.
  • The specifics: exact credentials, conditions you treat, insurance plans accepted, hours, and your booking link.

2. Get found locally (the biggest lever)

If you do only one thing, do this. Most people looking for care start with a quick online search, and a complete Google Business Profile is what puts you on the map, literally.

  • Claim and fully complete your Google Business Profile — services, hours, photos, neurofeedback, the works. Complete profiles get dramatically more clicks and calls than thin ones.
  • Keep your name, address, and phone identical everywhere (website, Google, Headway, Instagram). Google rewards consistency.
  • Aim to rank for the searches that matter: "medication management Virginia Beach," "psychiatric nurse practitioner Virginia Beach," "PTSD treatment for veterans Virginia Beach," and the niche term "neurofeedback Virginia Beach."

3. Lead with medication management and trauma care

These are your two front doors, and they should carry the site. Medication management meets the highest-volume, highest-intent demand in the area: a thorough psychiatric evaluation, thoughtful prescribing when it fits, and steady follow-up. Frame it honestly: medication is one option in a plan, it works best alongside therapy, and decisions are made collaboratively and measured ("start low, go slow").

Trauma care is your most defensible niche, especially for the PTSD and veteran community, delivered through trauma-focused therapy (including Brainspotting) plus medication support, with real discretion for anyone with career or clearance concerns.

Neurofeedback and Brainspotting are complementary, not the headline. Because almost no one local offers neurofeedback, a clear, plain-English page still helps you get found, but it now reads as a drug-free option offered alongside medication and therapy, and Brainspotting reads as one of your trauma-therapy methods. Keep them, just don't lead with them.

4. Build a few referral relationships

Word of mouth is the highest-quality, lowest-cost growth there is. A handful of warm relationships compounds quietly:

  • Therapists and counselors who need a prescriber to refer to (a natural fit with your trauma-informed approach).
  • Primary care and OB/GYN offices with long psychiatry wait lists.
  • The military and veteran community, quietly. This is the human core of the practice. With your Headstrong Project work, trauma-focused care, and real discretion (which matters to anyone with security-clearance or career concerns), Hampton Roads' large service-member population is a natural, high-trust niche. Build it discreetly through Headstrong, the VA, and military-friendly therapists, never by advertising names.
  • One coffee or intro a week is plenty. Slow and genuine beats a blast.

5. Reviews, Instagram, and Headway

  • Reviews: a steady trickle of real Google reviews is persuasive, but patient privacy comes first. Use passive, non-clinical review prompts and ask permitted professional contacts directly; do not pressure current clients or respond in a way that confirms someone is a patient.
  • Instagram (@anchorneurofeedback): you already have it. A light, regular cadence (a calming tip, a bit about neurofeedback, a glimpse of the space) keeps you visible without being a second job.
  • Headway: your profile there is itself a place clients discover you. Make it as complete and warm as the website.

6. Growing into a group practice

You're stepping out from Fearless Care to build your own practice, which is the exciting part. Right now it's just you; the goal is to add an office administrator and other nurse practitioners over time. That's very doable, and it has a specific shape worth planning around in Virginia:

  • As of July 2024, Virginia NPs can practice autonomously after about three years (roughly 5,400 hours) of qualifying experience. Before that threshold, an NP practices under a practice agreement with a collaborating physician.
  • So "NPs working under you" really means building a group practice where each clinician is either autonomous or set up with a collaborating physician the practice arranges. You'd be the owner and clinical lead, with shared systems, scheduling, and billing.
  • Bring clinicians on quietly through your network and a simple "now hiring" note when you're ready, rather than a public recruiting page, which keeps the site simple and focused on clients for now.
  • Keep the back end simple. One easy platform (for example SimplePractice) for scheduling, intake, notes, and billing, with Headway handling insurance, gives clients a smooth on-ramp now and scales cleanly when you add an admin and clinicians, with no tech headaches.
Worth confirming: the exact autonomous-practice timing and the collaborating-physician setup should be validated with the Virginia Board of Nursing and a healthcare attorney before you advertise hiring. The structure is the kind of thing that's simple once mapped, but you want it right.

A simple first 30 days

No need to do everything at once. A calm starting order:

  • Finalize and launch the website (add your bio, photos, credentials, insurance, booking link).
  • Claim and fully build out your Google Business Profile.
  • Create a passive Google review link or card, and ask permitted professional contacts for reviews without pressuring current patients.
  • Make sure your name/address/phone match across the website, Google, Headway, and Instagram.
  • Have one coffee or intro call with a local therapist or PCP office.
  • Post to Instagram once a week (keep it easy and human).
The short version: get the website live, get found on Google, lead with medication management and trauma care (with neurofeedback as a supporting option), collect reviews, and build relationships one at a time. That alone will grow the practice, and it sets the stage for adding clinicians when you're ready.
