Brainspotting

What is Brainspotting, and who is it for?

By Courtney Davis, DNP, APRN, PMHNP-BC, BCN · Updated June 2026

Brainspotting is a brain-body approach to processing trauma and distress, built on the idea that where your eyes rest can connect to where a feeling is held. Here is an honest look at what it is, who it tends to help, and what a session is actually like.

Draft, pending final review before publishing.

If you have ever felt that talking about something only takes you so far, you are not alone. Some experiences seem to live below words, in the body, in the nervous system, in the parts of the brain that language has trouble reaching. Brainspotting is one way of working with those deeper places. Below is a plain-language guide to what it is and who it can help, written for people considering care here in Virginia Beach and across Hampton Roads.

What Brainspotting is

Brainspotting is a brain-body approach to processing trauma and distress. It rests on a simple observation: the direction your eyes rest in can connect to where a feeling or memory is held in your brain and body. By finding and gently holding that eye position, often called a brainspot, you give your brain a way to process at its own pace, working with deeper, body-based systems rather than only the thinking, talking part of the mind.

It was developed by David Grand, PhD, who noticed that certain eye positions seemed to unlock material that talk therapy struggled to shift. From there he built it into a structured, paced way of working. The approach is sometimes described as following the brain's own lead: you find the spot, and your brain does the work, while your therapist stays with you the whole time.

An honest note on the evidence

We want to be straight with you, because your trust matters more than any sales pitch. Brainspotting is a newer modality, and its research base is growing but still developing. Early studies and a lot of clinical experience are encouraging, and many people find it genuinely helpful. It is not a proven cure, and it is not a guarantee. We treat it as a tool, not a promise. Anchor Neuro coordinates it and refers you to a trusted, experienced therapist who provides it, as one part of a plan rather than something that has to carry everything on its own.

Who Brainspotting can help

People most often come to Brainspotting when something feels stuck, when a part of their story still has a grip on them, or when talking it through has only gone so far. It is used with a range of concerns, including:

  • Trauma and PTSD. Single events and the kind that builds up over time, including the after effects of service.
  • Anxiety. The persistent, body-level kind that does not fully settle even when you understand it.
  • Grief and loss. When a loss sits heavy and you are looking for a gentle way to carry it.
  • Performance blocks. The freeze or self-doubt that shows up in work, sport, or anything that asks a lot of you.

Brainspotting is also used in trauma work with veterans and service members, which is one reason it is among the trauma-therapy methods Anchor Neuro coordinates and connects you with. What a session is like

A Brainspotting session is calm, paced, and led by you. Nothing is forced, and your therapist moves only as fast as feels safe. Anchor Neuro coordinates the referral so the right trained therapist is matched to you. In broad strokes, a session tends to unfold like this:

  1. You get grounded. You settle in, and you and your therapist connect to what you want to work on, at a pace that feels right for that day.
  2. You find a spot. With your therapist's gentle guidance, you notice where your eyes naturally rest while you stay with a feeling or memory.
  3. Your brain does the work. You let your brain and body process from that spot, while your therapist stays with you and tracks how you are doing.
  4. You close gently. Your therapist brings the session to a steady finish and talks through what came up and what comes next.

You stay in control the entire time. You decide what to share and how far to go, and you can pause whenever you need to. The work is meant to feel safe, not overwhelming.

A few common questions

Is Brainspotting a proven cure for trauma or PTSD?

No, and we will not tell you otherwise. Brainspotting is a newer brain-body modality with a growing but still-developing research base. Many people, including veterans, find it helpful, but it is not a guaranteed cure. Anchor Neuro coordinates it as one part of an integrated, individualized plan and refers you to a trusted therapist who provides it, and we are honest about what it can and cannot do.

How is Brainspotting different from EMDR or talk therapy?

All three can help with trauma, but they work differently. Talk therapy works mostly through language and insight. EMDR uses a structured, back-and-forth eye movement protocol. Brainspotting holds a single eye position, or brainspot, and lets your brain and body process at their own pace from there. Which approach fits you depends on your history and goals, and that is something we decide together before we connect you with a trusted therapist.
