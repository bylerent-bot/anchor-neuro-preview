The basics

What is neurofeedback?

Neurofeedback is a non-invasive, medication-free approach that helps your brain learn to regulate itself. Small sensors rest on your scalp and read your brain's electrical activity. That is all they do. They read, they do not send anything in.

As you sit comfortably, the system turns that activity into gentle, real-time feedback, often through sound or a screen. When your brain drifts toward a calmer, more focused pattern, the feedback rewards it. Over repeated sessions, your brain practices finding those steadier rhythms on its own, the same way any skill becomes more natural with practice.

Neurofeedback is offered in-office only, in Virginia Beach. It is not available over telehealth, because the sensors and equipment have to be with you in the room.

How it works

Four steps, at your pace

  1. We listen first. A thorough look at your history, symptoms, and what you want to feel different. This is also where we tell you honestly whether neurofeedback is a reasonable fit for you.
  2. We map your brain's patterns. Sensors read your activity so we can see where things tend to run hot or stay stuck. It is completely painless, and nothing is sent into you.
  3. Your brain practices. Through relaxing, repeated sessions, your brain is gently guided toward healthier rhythms using real-time feedback.
  4. Change that can stick. With repetition, steadier patterns can become more familiar for your brain. We track progress with you and adjust as we go.

What the research supports

Honest about the evidence

We will never oversell what neurofeedback can do. Here is the straight version.

Research offers reasonable support for neurofeedback in attention and ADHD, and in regulating anxiety, stress, and sleep. For many people it can help quiet a mind that will not slow down, or take the edge off a constantly activated nervous system.

For trauma and PTSD, the evidence is emerging and promising rather than definitive, and we say so plainly. Neurofeedback works best as part of an integrated plan, and we will always be honest with you about what it can and cannot do. It is a tool we use alongside therapy and medication, not a cure and not a guarantee.

Questions, answered

Neurofeedback FAQ

Is neurofeedback safe?

Neurofeedback is non-invasive and medication-free. The sensors only read your brain's electrical activity. Nothing is sent into your brain, and no electrical current is passed into you. It is well tolerated by most people, including children and older adults. Because everyone is different, we review your history first and tell you honestly whether it is a reasonable fit for you, and what to realistically expect.

Does neurofeedback hurt?

No. There are no needles and nothing sharp. We place small sensors on your scalp with a bit of gel, you sit comfortably, and you watch or listen to feedback while your brain does the work. Most people find the sessions relaxing. The most you will usually feel is the sensors resting on your head.

How many neurofeedback sessions will I need?

It varies from person to person, and we will not promise you a number we cannot stand behind. Neurofeedback works through repetition, so it is a course of sessions rather than a single visit, often spread over several weeks. After your assessment we will give you an honest, individualized estimate and check in on progress along the way, adjusting as we go.

Is neurofeedback covered by insurance?

It depends on your plan. We are in-network for psychiatric care with Aetna, Anthem / HealthKeepers, BlueCross BlueShield, Carelon, Cigna / Evernorth, and Sentara. Coverage for neurofeedback specifically can differ, so we confirm your coverage or a clear self-pay rate before your first session, never as a surprise afterward.

Can neurofeedback help with PTSD or trauma?

For trauma and PTSD, the research on neurofeedback is emerging and promising rather than definitive, and we will always be straight with you about that. For many people it can support the nervous-system regulation that trauma work depends on. We use it as one part of an integrated, trauma-informed plan, alongside therapy and medication when those help, not as a stand-alone cure.
