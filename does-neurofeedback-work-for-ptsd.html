Does neurofeedback work for PTSD?

By Courtney Davis, DNP, APRN, PMHNP-BC, BCN · Updated June 2026

The honest answer is: it shows promise, but it is not a proven cure. Here is what neurofeedback actually is, what the research supports today, and how it can fit a trauma-informed plan in Virginia Beach.

Draft, pending final review before publishing.

If you live with PTSD, you have probably read big claims in both directions. Some sites promise that neurofeedback will rewire your brain and end your symptoms. Others dismiss it entirely. Neither extreme is fair to you. The truthful picture sits in the middle, and you deserve to hear it plainly before you spend time or money on anything.

So let's walk through it the way I would in the office: what neurofeedback is, what the research really supports, why it may still help in trauma care, and who it tends to fit.

What neurofeedback actually is

Neurofeedback is a non-invasive way of helping your brain practice self-regulation. We place small sensors on your scalp that read your brain's electrical activity. Nothing is sent into your brain, and no current is applied. The sensors only listen.

That activity is turned into real-time feedback, often a video, a sound, or a simple game that responds when your brain produces steadier, calmer patterns. Over a course of sessions, your brain gradually learns to favor those patterns on its own. Think of it less like a treatment done to you and more like training your nervous system can do with you. Because it relies on those scalp sensors, neurofeedback is in-office only, here at our Hilltop office in Virginia Beach.

What the research actually supports

I want to be careful and accurate here, because this is the part most websites get wrong.

The strongest evidence for neurofeedback is in attention and ADHD, and there is reasonable support for using it to help with anxiety, stress, and nervous-system regulation. For many people, that translates into a mind that slows down a little more easily and a body that does not stay stuck in high alert.

For PTSD specifically, the evidence is emerging and promising, not definitive. There is a growing body of research, and some of it is encouraging, but it is not yet large or consistent enough to call neurofeedback a proven or stand-alone treatment for trauma. Anyone who guarantees results, or promises a specific number of sessions to a cure, is getting ahead of what the science can honestly say.

That does not mean it is worthless for trauma. It means we use it as one tool, with realistic expectations, inside a larger plan.

Why it may still help in trauma care

Here is the logic that makes neurofeedback worth considering even while the PTSD research is still maturing. Trauma lives in the nervous system. So much of PTSD shows up as a body that will not settle: hypervigilance, a startle that fires too easily, sleep that will not come, a sense of being switched on all the time.

Real trauma work, whether that is talk therapy, EMDR, Brainspotting, or medication, tends to land better when your nervous system has some capacity to regulate. It is hard to process a painful memory when your body is convinced it is still in danger. By supporting that underlying regulation, neurofeedback may help create a little more steadiness for the rest of the work to happen. It is a supporting actor, not the lead.

How it fits an integrated, trauma-informed plan

At Anchor Neuro I do not offer neurofeedback as a cure or as a substitute for therapy and clinical judgment. I use it as part of an integrated plan that is built around you. Depending on your situation, that plan might include:

  • Psychiatric care and medication management when medication is appropriate and wanted, by telehealth anywhere in Virginia or in person.
  • Brainspotting, a brain-body approach to processing trauma that I am trained in. It is a newer modality with a still-developing research base, used as a tool, not a guaranteed fix.
  • Neurofeedback to support nervous-system regulation, in-office at Hilltop.
  • Coordination with your therapist if you are already doing trauma work, so the pieces reinforce each other instead of competing.

The goal is honest, layered care, not a single gadget carrying all the weight.

What to realistically expect

Neurofeedback is not a one-and-done session. It typically involves a course of regular sessions, and the right number is individualized. I will not promise you a fixed count or a guaranteed outcome, because no honest provider can. Some people notice changes in sleep or baseline calm before they notice anything else. Some respond well, and some do not respond much at all, which is true of every tool in mental health care.

What I can promise is that we will track how you are actually doing and adjust, rather than push a protocol that is not helping you.

Who it is, and is not, a fit for

Neurofeedback may be worth exploring if you are dealing with the kind of constant activation, poor sleep, anxiety, or difficulty settling that often travels with PTSD, and you want a non-invasive, medication-free piece to add to your care.

It is probably not the right starting point if you are in acute crisis, if you need or want to begin with medication or talk therapy, or if you are looking for a fast, stand-alone cure. In those cases we start somewhere more appropriate, and neurofeedback can come into the picture later if it fits. A free initial consult is the best way to sort out which path makes sense for you.

A few honest questions, answered

Is neurofeedback a proven cure for PTSD?

No. There is no proven cure for PTSD, and neurofeedback is not one. The research for neurofeedback in PTSD is emerging and promising, not definitive. It is best thought of as one supportive tool inside a trauma-informed plan, not a stand-alone treatment.

Does neurofeedback send anything into my brain?

No. The sensors only read your brain's electrical activity. Nothing is sent in, and no current is applied. You watch or listen to feedback that reflects your own activity in real time, and your brain gradually practices steadier patterns.

Can I do neurofeedback by telehealth?

No. Neurofeedback at Anchor Neuro is in-office only, at our Hilltop office in Virginia Beach, because it uses sensors on your scalp. Psychiatric care, medication management, and Brainspotting can be done by secure telehealth anywhere in Virginia or in person.
