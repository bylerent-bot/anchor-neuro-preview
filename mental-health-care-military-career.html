Getting mental health care in the military: what it means for your career

By Courtney Davis, DNP, APRN, PMHNP-BC, BCN · Updated June 2026

The fear that asking for help will cost you your career or your clearance is real, and it keeps a lot of good people from getting care they need. Here is an honest, general look at how this actually works, and how to get support privately in Virginia Beach or anywhere in Virginia.

Draft, pending final review before publishing.

If you are reading this, you have probably already weighed the cost. You know something is off, maybe sleep, maybe anger, maybe a numbness you cannot shake, and a voice in the back of your head keeps asking the same question: what happens to my career if I get help?

That fear is common, and it is understandable. The military runs on readiness, and asking for help can feel like admitting you are not. But here is the part that gets lost: avoiding needed care carries its own risk. Untreated symptoms tend to get louder, not quieter, and they show up in the places you are trying to protect, your performance, your relationships, your judgment. Getting ahead of that is the stronger move, not the weaker one.

Important: the clearance, command-notification, and duty-status details below are general information, not legal or security-clearance advice. The exact rules can depend on your branch, your role, and your situation, and they change over time. Confirm anything that affects your specific case with your security officer, your command, or a JAG before you rely on it.

The honest answer on your security clearance

Let's start with the worry that stops the most people. In general, seeking mental health treatment by itself rarely affects security clearance eligibility. Military and national-security leaders have said this directly and repeatedly: getting help is treated as a sign of good judgment, not a disqualifier. The thing that more often raises questions on a clearance review is an untreated, unaddressed problem, not the fact that someone went and dealt with it.

The security questionnaire itself reflects this. On the SF-86, Question 21, you are not required to disclose counseling in three specific situations:

  • Counseling that was strictly for marital, family, or grief reasons and was not court-ordered
  • Care that was strictly related to adjustments from service in a military combat environment
  • Care related to being a victim of sexual assault

Those carve-outs exist on purpose, to make it easier, not harder, to get help. That said, this is general information, and your circumstances are your own. How any of it applies to you is a question for your security officer or a JAG, not for a website.

What about confidentiality and your command?

When you see a private, civilian provider, your care is protected by HIPAA, the same federal privacy law that covers everyone. Your records are not handed to your command simply because you walked in the door. There are narrow legal and safety exceptions that apply to all patients, civilian and military alike, mainly situations involving an imminent risk of serious harm, but those are the exception, not the rule, and a good provider will be upfront with you about them.

Care inside the military health system works differently in some cases. Rules around active-duty care and command notification can depend on your duty status, your role, and the setting you are seen in. This is exactly the kind of specific that is worth confirming for your situation rather than assuming.

Specifics that should be confirmed with your security officer, command, or a JAG: exact security-clearance and SF-86 reporting requirements for your role, when and whether your command would be notified, and any clearance-policy or duty-status rules that apply to active-duty care. We cannot promise how these apply to you.

If you are in crisis, reach out now. Call the Veterans Crisis Line by dialing 988 and pressing 1, or text 838255. It is free, confidential, and available to all service members, veterans, and their families, around the clock. You do not have to be enrolled in VA care to use it.

Why a private path can feel safer

One reason service members come to a practice like Anchor Neuro is privacy. Seeing a civilian provider in the community, on your own time, can feel like a lower-stakes way to start. Many people want to talk to someone before anything becomes a formal part of their record, and they want room to figure out what they are dealing with first.

Secure telehealth makes that easier. You can meet from home, from your car, or from any private spot, anywhere in Virginia, which means no waiting room, no sign-in sheet, and a schedule that bends around duty rather than the other way around. Questions service members ask

Will my command find out if I get mental health care?

It depends on where and how you get care. Care from a private, civilian provider is protected by HIPAA and is generally not reported to your command on its own, though the narrow legal and safety exceptions that apply to everyone still apply. Inside the military health system, command-notification rules can depend on your duty status, your role, and the situation. Because the specifics vary, confirm how this works in your case with your security officer, command, or a JAG.

Does getting mental health treatment hurt my security clearance?

In general, seeking mental health treatment by itself rarely affects security clearance eligibility, and military leaders have repeatedly encouraged getting help. The SF-86, Question 21, also does not require you to disclose counseling that was strictly non-court-ordered marital, family, or grief counseling, strictly combat-related care, or care related to being a victim of sexual assault. This is general information, not a guarantee for any individual. Confirm how it applies to you with your security officer or a JAG.

Is telehealth really a private way to get care?

Secure telehealth lets you meet from home or another private spot anywhere in Virginia, which many service members find easier to fit around duty schedules and more discreet than sitting in a waiting room. The same confidentiality protections and the same narrow legal and safety exceptions apply.
